The Starting Point

One Program Doesn't Fit All

I teach founders at San Diego State to build businesses. For years, the path has been the same: identify a problem → validate through sales → build the solution.

That works for non-technical founders. They don't have the luxury of building first, so they have to validate first.

But I kept wondering: is that the right path for technical founders?

3/6
The Realization

Asymmetric Access = Asymmetric Insight

Technical founders using cutting-edge AI have something most people don't: they're living in the future.

Maybe 0.1% of the population has real exposure to agent orchestration, local inference, stateful memory systems, multi-agent coordination.

"If you have access to tools that 99.9% of people don't, you can see problems that normal people don't even know they're about to have."

That's a fundamentally different starting point than traditional founders have.

4/6
The Shift

Two Different Validation Questions

Non-Technical Path
  • Find a problem
  • Validate demand exists
  • Build the solution
  • Ask: "Will people pay?"
Technical Path
  • Work at the frontier
  • Feel the friction firsthand
  • Build the wedge now
  • Ask: "Is this inevitable?"

Technical founders don't validate demand. They validate inevitability.

5/6
The Framework

Four Friction Patterns to Watch

Capability Shock
New AI capability appears → old workflows instantly break
Abundance Inversion
Something goes from scarce to free → new frictions emerge
Delegation Overload
Tasks become delegable → bottleneck shifts to coordination, memory, evaluation
Human-In-the-Loop Bottleneck
AI creates workflows → humans still stuck approving, labeling, deciding
6/6
Where This Lands

The "Live in the Future" Program

I'm developing a separate track for technical founders at SDSU:

Weekly frontier exposure — hands-on with capabilities most people won't touch for years.

Friction logging — not brainstorming, but documenting what actually breaks.

Inevitability arguments — why this problem is guaranteed to exist in 12-36 months.

The goal: founders who are 18-24 months ahead of the market, building wedges into problems that don't fully exist yet — but will.
